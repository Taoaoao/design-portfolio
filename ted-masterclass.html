TED Masterclass

A journey from B2B to B2C

RoleLead Product Designer, full time

PeriodJuly 2019 – Present

Overview

In July 2019, I worked with the TED-Ed team to iterate on the TED Masterclass app. Back then, it was a B2B app for educators and corporate users to improve their public speaking skills. To grow the user base, the team decided to transition the app into a B2C product. Four months later, we successfully launched on iOS and Android. Since then, there has been consistent growth in user base and revenue, and the app is rated 4.4 in the App Store.

Design challenges

The course content was adapted from Chris Anderson's bestselling book "The Official TED Guide to Public Speaking." The content would become TED's first priced product in the app stores. Because TED has been known for free access to great ideas, we had to be mindful of the pricing strategy and what it meant in terms of user experience.

Challenge 1

What's the right price point for the B2C app? Can we leverage user research to help identify the sweet spot?

Challenge 2

Which model is right for us—a purchase-to-download app or an app with a freemium experience? What is the freemium experience for us?

User research

Given that the price point question would be biased in a user research setting, the team decided to test the market reaction by setting an initial price. We surveyed and interviewed people who had registered but didn't purchase the course.

The goals of the research were to: (a) identify a reasonable price point from the users' perspective; (b) understand people's top considerations for purchase; (c) identify other areas of opportunity.

Findings

Over 70% of survey respondents rated "Price" as the top consideration preventing purchase. In the follow-up question "How much do you think the course is worth?", respondents gave a range of $30–50.

We also realized that local purchasing power is an extremely important purchase factor after talking to a student in India.

Implications

Informed by the research, the team ran a $49.99 price point experiment. The result led us to settle on a new price point. With Google Play Store, we adjusted pricing for 10 global markets—resulting in an 18× increase in conversion rate in those regions.

Product iterations and testing

At one point, there was an internal debate about whether a pay-to-download or an in-app purchase model would be more successful. I conducted research and prepared a list of pros and cons for discussion. After thoughtful evaluation, we moved forward with in-app purchase—which required putting more weight on the pre-purchase in-app experience than upfront App Store marketing.

Iteration 1 — Paywalls

To maximize conversion, it's important to provide "just enough" value before people decide to buy. I ran usability testing to see if people understood that they had to make an in-app purchase to proceed. We added an explanatory paragraph to clarify the value of TED's curation insights.

Iteration 2 — Freemium experience

What should we make free? TED Masterclass consists of 11 lessons, each with a short preview video. Initially, only previews were available for free. User interviews revealed that previews weren't telling the full story. I suggested A/B testing against having Lesson 1 fully free. The result led us to adopt Lesson 1 as the free entry point.

Iteration 3 — Web app

Repetitive user feedback: typing long learning reflections on a phone is cumbersome. I strongly believed making TED Masterclass accessible on the web would improve the experience significantly and open new business opportunities. Post-launch survey: 82% of respondents said they would do the rest of the course almost or entirely on a computer.

Design system

Reflections

Make user research a regular team effort. Good design is to advocate and optimize for the user experience given all business and engineering constraints. It won't happen without a shared understanding of our customers. I invited product, marketing, and business team members to the research process—a very valuable practice for all.

Localization should not be an afterthought. Due to the nature of the TED brand, we reach international users even when we didn't expect to. It taught me that inclusive design can come in different shapes and forms (in this case, localized pricing), and localization should never be an afterthought in the design process.
